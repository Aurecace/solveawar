Reported speech

What is reported speech?

Reported speech is the language that we use when we tell someone what another person said (or thought!). To do this, we can use direct speech or indirect speech.

Example: Your friend Isabel invites you to her home. But your father grounded you because you recently failed a test.

Your answer to Isabel could be...

  • Direct speech: I can't go, Isabel. My dad said "you're grounded".
  • Indirect speech: I can't go, Isabel. My dad says I'm grounded.

Direct speech is easy to learn, but indirect speech is usually the most natural way to speak. How do we form it?

Verb tenses

To form indirect speech, we first need to take a look at the verb. If the sentence speaks about the present or the future, we don't need to change it. However, if the sentence speaks about the past, we need to adapt what the original person said.

Example: if Isabel is inviting you now...

  1. Isabel asks "Do you want to come over?" (PRESENT)
  2. You ask your dad if you can go out.
  3. He says "You are grounded".
  4. You tell Isabel "Dad says I am grounded".

If Isabel invited you yesterday...

  1. She asks "Why didn't you come yesterday?" (PAST)
  2. Your dad said "You are grounded".
  3. Yo tell Isabel "Dad said I was grounded".

Do you get it?

If we want to pass a sentence about the past to indirect speech, we change the verb tense like this:

Present simple ------------------> Past simple
Present continuous -------------> Past continuous
Present perfect -----------------> Past perfect

We also adapt sentences that were originally in a past tense (for example, if your friend says "I thought you were grounded"). But we won't study that for now.

Examples:

PRESENT PAST
My dad tells me "you are grounded" He told me I was grounded.
My dad tells me "you're not studying". He told me I was not studying.
My dad tells me "you haven't done your homework He told me I hadn't done my homework.

REMEMBER!

Modal verbs also have a past form. We have already studied them.

Examples:

PRESENT PAST
My dad tells me "you can fail the test"

He told me I can fail the test (you still haven't done it).

He told me I could fail the test (you already did the test).
My dad tells me "you will fail the test".

He told me I will fail the test (you still haven't done it).

He told me I would fail the test (you already did the test).

Think about it...

You can say the same message with two completely different sentences. For example, if you want to tell your friend that you can't go shopping with him next Saturday, you can tell him :

  • "I can't, man. I have to study"
  • "I can't, man. I will be studying"

The two sentences say the same message. Of course, there are small differences, but both options are correct. Each person speaks in a different way. That doesn't mean they are wrong. Language is personal. You just have to be sure that the rest of the people understand you!

Activity: Multi-select

Question

Question 1

Your friend Mary believes that your English teacher set a test next Friday. But you think she is wrong. How can you tell her?

Answers

  • You're wrong. The teacher said the test is next Monday.

  • You're wrong. The teacher said the test was being next Monday.

  • You're wrong! The teacher said the test was next Monday.

  • You're wrong! The teacher said the test will be next Monday.

Feedback

We're speaking about a future event (next Monday), but the teacher told you about the test in the past. As you can see, you can say the same thing in different correct ways. Think about it in Spanish. Can you also say the same with different verb tenses?

Solution

  1. Correct
  2. Incorrect
  3. Incorrect
  4. Correct

  • Sorry! My mom says I have to study, so I can't go.

  • Sorry! My mom said I was studying, so I couldn't go.

There's only one correct choice this time.

Question

Question 3

A colleague from school, Ethan is speaking about the birthday party of your friend David, which still hasn't been confirmed. He says David has cancelled the party. But you know that is not true because the last time you spoke to David he told you "I'm going to ask my mother for permission". How do you correct Ethan?

Answers

  • That's not true. David told me he was going to ask his mother.

  • That's not true. David told me he had to ask his mother.

  • That's not true. David told me he asked his mother.

  • That's not true. David told me he is going to ask his mother.

Feedback

As you can see, you say the same message with two different sentences:

  • He had to ask his mother = Tenía que preguntarle a su madre.
  • He was going to ask his mother = Iba a preguntarle a su madre.

You just have to respect the tenses so the message makes sense! The last option could also be valid if you know that David still hasn't talked to his mom.

Solution

  1. Correct
  2. Correct
  3. Incorrect
  4. Incorrect

Question

Question 4

Remember we can also use indirect speech to speak about thoughts.

Imagine that you are nervous about a song contest next Saturday. You and your best friend Ella will participate together. Your dad asks you "Have you talked to Ella? How is she feeling about the contest?". What do you answer?

Answers

  • I spoke to her. She thinks we will win the contest.

  • I'm speaking to her. She thought we will win the contest.

  • I spoke to her. She thought we will win the contest.

  • I will speak to her. She thinks we will win the contest.

Feedback

If you know Ella's opinion, you already spoke to her (in the past). But her opinion is about something from the future, so you have to use a future tense.

Solution

  1. Correct
  2. Incorrect
  3. Incorrect
  4. Incorrect

Question 5

You read an article from 2023 about the war between Russia and Ukraine. An expert says the following sentence:

  • "The Russo-Ukrainian war is affecting the economy of the two countries".

How do you share this information with another person?

Answers

  • In 2023, an expert said that the Russo-Ukrainian war is affecting the economy of both countries. 

  • In 2023, an expert said that the Russo-Ukrainian war affects the economy of both countries. 

  • In 2023, an expert said that the Russo-Ukrainian war was affecting the economy of both countries. 

  • In 2023, an expert said that the Russo-Ukrainian war has affected the economy of both countries.

Remember, if you are passing an exact sentence about the past to indirect speech, you have to respect the table we have seen before. Of course, there are multiple ways of saying the same if you don't want to copy exactly the same sentence.

Can you think of other ways to say the same using a different tense?

  2. Incorrect
  3. Correct
  4. Incorrect

Question

Question 6

Which of the following reported speech sentences make sense?

Answers

  • Last week, Jeff said he won't come to the party.

  • My dad told me he's grounded so I can't go with you.

  • Mom thought he would be more beautiful with this suit on.

  • Mom thought she would be more beautiful with the dress on.

Feedback

As you can see, pronouns, places and things don't need to refer to the same thing. These two last sentences obviously mean totally different things!

  1. Incorrect
  2. Incorrect
  3. Correct
  4. Correct

Reported speech – Adapting information

We've seen that, first of all, you must pay attention to verb tenses so the message makes sense when you pass it to indirect speech. Now, think about the following sentence:

  • My sister said "I have to buy mountain boots in this shop because I need them for tomorrow".

If you pass this sentence to indirect speech and only switch the verb tense, you will have this:

  • My sister said I had to buy mountain boots in this shop because I needed them for tomorrow.

Does it sound right to you? No, it doesn't. Because the sentence is not speaking about you, but about your sister. So you have to make changes for it to make sense. If you think about it, in Spanish we do the same!

Imagine that, a week after, you are speaking to a friend about what your sister said. A correct way to pass this sentence to indirect speech could be:

  • My sister said she had to buy mountain boots in that shop because she needed them for the day after.

As you can see, we must change the following parts:

  • The pronoun (we're speaking about "my sister", so we use "she").
  • The place (We are not in "this shop" at the moment, so we have to speak about a place that is far away. We can say "the shop", "that shop", we can use the name of the shop, etc.
  • The moment (When I'm using reported speech y sister has already gone to the mountain, so she doesn't need the boots for tomorrow).

But what if you met your friend the day after in the same shop you visited with your sister?

Then you could also say:

  • My sister said she had to buy mountain boots in this shop because she needed them for today.

Do you get it?
